The problem

Apple Health is a silo.

Your watch knows more about your body than any doctor. Getting that data out — and doing something smart with it — is an unsolved engineering problem.

🔒

Export is broken by design

Apple lets you export a ZIP. The XML inside is 500 MB, unstructured, and missing half your workout data. Shortcuts can drip samples one-by-one, but there's no streaming API.

🔌

Third-party collectors are a privacy risk

Every "sync to Google Sheets" app is a new data custodian. Your HRV, sleep stages, and resting heart rate end up on someone else's server, often unencrypted.

🧩

The wiring problem

Even if you get the data out, you still need a receiver, a transformer, a store, a query layer, and something to reason over it. Most people stop at the export.

💬

No conversational interface

Charts are fine. But "should I train hard today?" requires context: HRV trend, last night's sleep, what you have on your calendar. No app cross-references all of that.

The insight

Compile a Pilot daemon into the iOS app. Make the iPhone a network node.

Instead of exporting data out of the phone, we run a Go network daemon — pilot-swift — inside the iOS app sandbox. The iPhone becomes a first-class node on an encrypted overlay network. Health samples are pushed directly to an agent with no HTTP server, no port forwarding, no third party in the middle. The tunnel is E2E encrypted and NAT-traversed. It just works.

The solution

Two agents. Each owns its data.

We built two OpenClaw agents — each with its own memory, its own G-Brain, and a single shared communication channel. They talk through Pilot Protocol. You talk to them on Telegram.

Agent A · Collector

Retains your health data

Receives HealthKit envelopes from the iPhone, deduplicates by sample UUID, and writes to a DuckDB warehouse. Answers SQL queries from the Coach. Its G-Brain holds what actually happened — factual observations, no interpretation.

Owns
  • facts.duckdb — every sample ever received
  • gbrain-collector-home — factual memory
  • Ack logic — the iPhone only advances its cursor after confirmation
  • 84 unit tests, 8 E2E scenarios
Agent B · Coach

Retains what you did

Monitors health events, runs 7 rule models, pulls your Google Calendar, and reasons over all of it. Sends you proactive nudges on Telegram when something needs your attention. Answers questions with paper-backed evidence.

Owns
  • gbrain-coach-home — interpretive memory, prior nudges
  • Calendar context — daily markdown via Google OAuth
  • 7 rule models — HRV, burnout, sleep, circadian drift, and more
  • Telegram interface via OpenClaw channel binding

How it works

From wrist to answer.

Every step is traceable. No magic, no cloud middleware.

1

Apple Watch samples HealthKit (27 on-device models)

Anchored queries capture every sample exactly once — HRV, sleep stages, resting heart rate, SpO₂, training load, and 22 more. 27 on-device models compute composites like burnout CUSUM and circadian drift. No server dependency for analysis.

2

pilot-swift packages and sends the envelope

The embedded Go daemon calls pilotctl send-message <collector-node-id> --data '<json>'. The envelope carries a batch UUID, sample UUIDs, and raw values. The tunnel is E2E encrypted — no data visible to any relay.

3

Collector receives, deduplicates, confirms

The JSON lands in the Collector's Pilot inbox. inbox_watcher.py classifies by content shape — a message with a samples array goes to the ingest path. INSERT OR IGNORE on UUID deduplicates. An Ack is sent back to the iPhone; only then does it advance the HealthKit cursor. Crash-safe by design.

4

Collector notifies Coach via ChangeEvent

After each batch commit, a {kind: "samples_added"} message is sent to the Coach's inbox. Same channel, different content shape. No pub/sub infrastructure — just send-message.

5

Coach runs 7 rule models; queries the Collector's DuckDB

On each ChangeEvent, the Coach sends a SQL query message to the Collector. The Collector executes it read-only and replies with a QueryResult. The Coach correlates replies by request_id. If a rule fires — HRV below baseline, burnout CUSUM crossing a threshold, circadian drift — and cooldown has elapsed, a Telegram nudge goes out.

6

You ask a question on Telegram. The Coach answers.

The Coach combines a DuckDB query, G-Brain recall, and health-intelligence RAG — 17 peer-reviewed papers, 89 interventions — to answer in ≤200 words with inline citations. Calendar context from Google OAuth is already in its G-Brain, so "HRV dropped during my heavy meeting week" is answerable without you explaining the context.

The bigger idea

Add another agent. Share its node ID. Done.

Each agent is a fully independent unit — its own OpenClaw workspace, its own Pilot identity, its own G-Brain, its own Docker container. They don't share a database or a network socket. The only thing they share is the ability to call send-message to each other's node ID.

G-Brains can communicate without a lot of shared priors — they converge on meaning through message exchange. The more agents you add, the more data they pool, the better they reason. This is the architecture: distributed data, distributed compute, federated via Pilot.
